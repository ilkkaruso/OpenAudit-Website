OpenAudit Philippines

Methodology & Data Documentation

Overview

OpenAudit Philippines is a transparency initiative that visualizes audit compliance data for Local Government Units (LGUs) across the Philippines. The project analyzes Commission on Audit (COA) Annual Audit Reports to track whether LGUs implement recommendations made by government auditors.

The goal is to provide citizens, researchers, journalists, and policymakers with accessible information about government accountability at the local level.

Data Source

Commission on Audit (COA) Annual Audit Reports

The Commission on Audit is the supreme audit institution of the Philippines, mandated by the 1987 Constitution to examine and audit all government agencies, including local government units. Each year, COA releases Annual Audit Reports (AARs) for every LGU containing:

  • Audit observations - Issues identified during the audit
  • Recommendations - Specific actions the LGU should take to address issues
  • Management actions - The LGU's response or actions taken
  • Status of prior year's recommendations - Whether previous recommendations were implemented

Data Extraction Process

Audit observations were extracted from COA AARs using a combination of PDF parsing and natural language processing. The status of each recommendation was classified into one of the following categories:

Status Count Description
IMPLEMENTED 79,798 Recommendation was fully addressed by the LGU
PARTIALLY IMPLEMENTED 60,135 Some action taken but recommendation not fully addressed
NOT IMPLEMENTED 61,509 No meaningful action taken on the recommendation
ONGOING 1,472 Implementation in progress (treated as partial)
UNKNOWN 185,283 Status could not be determined from available data

Note: Observations with UNKNOWN status are excluded from scoring calculations. Only observations with a determinable implementation status contribute to an LGU's risk score.

Scoring Methodology

Each LGU receives a Risk Score from 0 to 100, where higher scores indicate greater non-compliance risk. The score combines three factors:

Risk Score = (Base Rate × 0.40) + (Streak Penalty × 0.30) + (Trend Score × 0.30)

Factor 1: Base Rate (40% weight)

The base rate measures the overall proportion of recommendations that were not implemented, using weighted values:

  • NOT_IMPLEMENTED = 1.0 (full weight)
  • PARTIALLY_IMPLEMENTED = 0.5 (half weight)
  • IMPLEMENTED = 0.0 (no weight)
Base Rate = (NOT_IMPLEMENTED + 0.5 × PARTIALLY_IMPLEMENTED) / Total Known

This rate (0.0 to 1.0) is converted to a 0-100 scale for the composite calculation.

Factor 2: Streak Penalty (30% weight)

This factor penalizes LGUs that have shown sustained non-compliance over consecutive years. A "bad year" is defined as having a weighted non-compliance rate greater than 40%.

The exponential penalty structure reflects the increased concern when an LGU demonstrates a persistent pattern of non-compliance rather than isolated incidents.

Factor 3: Trend (30% weight)

The trend factor captures whether an LGU's compliance is improving or deteriorating over time. It compares the average non-compliance rate in recent years (2022-2024) versus earlier years (2016-2019).

Trend Change = Recent Rate (2022-2024) − Early Rate (2016-2019)
  • Improving: Change < -5 percentage points
  • Stable: Change between -5 and +5 percentage points
  • Worsening: Change > +5 percentage points

The trend change is scaled to a score from -50 (major improvement) to +50 (major deterioration), then centered at 25 for the composite calculation.

LGU Coverage & Data Gaps

As of 2024, the Philippines has 1,493 municipalities and 149 cities, for a total of 1,642 city/municipal LGUs. This dataset contains audit data for 1,351 LGUs (82.3% coverage).

Why Are Some LGUs Missing?

The 291 LGUs without data in this dataset are missing due to several factors:

📄

PDF Extraction Failures

Some COA Annual Audit Reports use scanned images, non-standard formatting, or corrupted PDF structures that prevent automated text extraction. These reports exist but could not be processed programmatically.

🔗

PSGC Matching Issues

LGU names in audit reports sometimes differ from official PSGC registry names (e.g., "Sta. Rosa" vs "Santa Rosa", local names vs official names). Some LGUs could not be matched to their geographic codes.

📁

Missing Source Documents

Not all COA Annual Audit Reports are available in the online COA portal. Some years or LGUs may have reports that were not digitized or uploaded.

🆕

Newly Created LGUs

Some municipalities or cities were created after 2016 through conversion or separation from existing LGUs, resulting in incomplete historical data.

Limitations & Caveats

  • Data extraction accuracy: Automated extraction from PDF documents may introduce errors. Some status classifications may be incorrect.
  • Unknown status observations: 47.7% of observations have unknown status. These are excluded from calculations but their exclusion may bias results if the unknown status is systematically related to non-compliance.
  • Not a measure of corruption: Non-implementation of audit recommendations does not necessarily indicate corruption. It may reflect capacity constraints, resource limitations, or disagreement with recommendations.
  • Geographic coverage: While 1,351 LGUs have data, some LGUs may be missing or have incomplete coverage across all years.
  • Year 2022 gap: The 2022 data has significantly fewer observations, which may affect trend calculations for some LGUs.
  • Recommendation severity: All recommendations are weighted equally. A minor procedural recommendation and a major financial control recommendation contribute the same to the score.

Geographic Coding (PSGC)

The Philippine Standard Geographic Code (PSGC) is the official coding system used by the Philippine Statistics Authority for geographic identification. Each LGU has a unique 10-digit code:

03 02 14 000
Region Province City/Mun Barangay

This coding system enables joining audit data with official geographic boundaries for map visualization.
